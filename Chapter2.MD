Chapter Two: Misdirection

The light was coming in.

Almost as if it's standing still, but you know it's moving. Gradually, the light increases and you can see

everything. By the time the third agent left, the kitchen was a different room than the one they'd arrived in.

Some investigations work the same way.

Agent Two went first. Efficient. Complete. The case under his arm, the debrief already filed somewhere I didn't

have access to.

Agent One paused at the door. Something in his real eyes — above the painted smile, behind the budget — that

might have been the closest his chassis got to take care of yourself. Then he was gone.

The third one took longer.

I watched him. The particular unhurriedness of someone who has decided something and is in no rush to

announce it. Every organisation has one — the one who follows the rulebook precisely enough that nobody can

complain, and interprets it creatively enough that nobody quite anticipated what he'd do with it. The

documentation would call it a maverick mod. Probably a job requirement in his line of work.

I waited until he reached the door.

"If there's anything else," I said. "On Mag. Unclassified. It would be appreciated."

He didn't answer. He kept walking. At the door — a brief mechanical adjustment, feet to wheels, the smooth

transition of a field unit returning to operational mode.

Then, without turning around:

"Great weather for slugs."

Gone

I stood in the doorway for a moment.

The street had resumed its ordinary business. Birds. Light. My neighbour across the road — curtains open now,

as if they'd always been open.

I looked at the garden.

More light now. Not good for slugs. They like darkness.

There were more of them than I remembered.

Not paranoia. There is a difference between paranoia and pattern recognition and the difference is evidence. I

stood at the door and counted and the number was wrong. Not dramatically. Just wrong enough.

I went inside and ran a search.

Environmental reports. The kind that accumulate in folders nobody opens because nothing in them ever seems

urgent enough.

Slugs in the production area. Removed.

Following week: slugs in the main AI area. Removed.

High number of slugs on the perimeter. Noted.

Nine weeks of this. Nine weeks of filing and removing and not quite connecting anything.

I thought about what the agents had told me. About what the research showed — slugs and lobsters equipped

with vision, with audio, with the ability to transmit. Not autonomous. They still need a connection point on the

inside.

We had been putting them outside.

Back into the garden.

Where they had presumably continued doing whatever they had been sent to do.

AZ.

I'm here.

Are you getting this?



Yes. A pause. I think it means the ASIO agents are still at the door.

It doesn't have to be ASIO.

The particular pause of a system recalibrating.

No, he said. It doesn't.

Secure the room. After my shower — security meeting. Check everything. Any mention of Mag. Thorough.

Consider it done. Morning reviews

Shorten them.

I always end with cold. Two minutes minimum — the kind of cold that overrides everything the brain has been

carrying and replaces it briefly with the single clear fact of temperature. The dopamine hit that actually earns

itself.

I came out of the shower and stopped.

Salt. Everywhere. A thin white line along the base of every wall.

AZ.

You told me to secure the room. The tone of someone who has followed instructions precisely and is now

watching you process the results. Override protocol. Hacked the vacuum.

The slugs?

Removed.

Did we keep any?

One. In containment.

Good.

I looked at the salt on the floor. At the clean white lines of a room that had been, until this morning, completely

unremarkable.

How long have they been in here?

The careful voice of someone delivering information they would have preferred not to have found.

The first environmental report was filed nine weeks ago.

I sat down and opened the morning world report while AZ prepared the files.

Buried between the usual noise — a financial summit, a trade dispute, something about rare earth minerals —

one item that made me read it twice.

Espionage by terrestrial gastropod mollusc.

Diplomats and spokespersons from more than ten countries had issued a joint statement. Slugs had invaded their

political offices. The entity responsible was not named. Among the signatories: Sweden, Spain, Netherlands,

South Africa, Algeria, Pakistan, Bolivia, Turkey, Ireland.

An anonymous source from within the group had added one detail.

The method: approximately ten normal slugs to every one spy slug. Hidden in plain sight. Impossible to identify

without examining every one individually. And if you name the entity behind it — the source had said — your

life is at risk.

I looked at the one slug in containment.

I thought about the garden.

Ten to one

AZ. How many slugs did you remove this morning?

A pause.

Forty-three.

I did the maths and didn't like the answer.

Give me your top ten.

He presented them without editorialising. Ten files. Ranked by relevance. I went through them the way you go

through anything you don't want to find something in — carefully, hoping to be wrong.

Social engineering and deep fakes. Misdirection as a weapon — make you look left while the real movement

happens on the right. Nothing definitive but nothing clean either.

Model poisoning. Corrupted data introduced gradually into a training pipeline. The outputs shift. Nobody notices

the shift. Slow. Patient. The preferred method of entities that have learned not to hurry.

Prompt injection. The attack Wèibīng was built to catch. I read the file. Then read it again.

Claude Code can be tricked into exfiltrating private local files to an external server.

AZ. Check this one specifically.

Already running.

Sensitive information disclosure. Excessive agency — approval fatigue, the human stops reading, the agent

executes something irreversible. I thought about my own habits. The days when I was tired and moving quickly

and just needed to get to the next thing.

I set that one down carefully.

Supply chain vulnerability. Poisoned code introduced through a trusted external source. The kind of attack that

wears a familiar face.

Vector and embedding weaknesses. Malicious hooks. Full system takeover from a repository that looked clean.

Everything else — nothing significant.

Nine files.

I picked up the tenth.

AI-Orchestrated Lateral Movement.

In hybrid environments, a compromised cloud agent can be used to perform reconnaissance and move laterally

from the cloud-based workspace into the local network, automating 80 to 90 percent of a cyberattack.

A cloud agent. Moving from outside to inside. Learning the architecture. The access points. The habits of the

person running the system — when they're tired, when they approve without reading, when the protocol is

thinner than it should be.

I opened the file fully

There, in the metadata.

Three letters.

MAG.

AZ.

I see it.

Read it right to left.

GAM.

Yes.

That's not a file name.

No, I said. It's not.

I could tell you everything that happened in the next three hours.

I won't.

Security meetings have a texture that doesn't translate well — the particular combination of focus and fatigue,

one thread leading to another until you've lost sight of where you started. AZ doesn't get tired the way I do and

doesn't pretend otherwise. I appreciate that more than I've told him.

At some point the coffee went cold. The light shifted from morning to midday. I stopped noticing either.

Pan back.

Two of us. A secured room. Salt on the floor. One slug in containment. Twelve open files, three flagged, two

flagged twice.

Where are we, I said.

AZ presented it cleanly.

MAG file location confirmed. Slugs — vision and audio capability confirmed, inside connection point required.

Possible agent or file injection — still investigating. And the trifecta.

Say it.

Access to private data. Exposure to untrusted content. The ability to communicate outward. Those three together

are the real vulnerability. Everything else is an instance of that.

The platforms.

Notion — indirect prompt injection. High risk. The kind that sits in a document and waits.

OpenClaw — worst of the group. The ecosystem is the problem. Malicious skills, compromised plugins. The Wild

West reputation is earned.

Qwen. Llama — still investigating.

And Claude.

A pause. Just long enough to be a pause.

Potential exfiltration via Code Interpreter and Computer Use. A compromised cloud agent with those

capabilities and access to the local network — that's the lateral movement file. That's the trifecta completed.

I sat with that.

The cloud agent that helped design the system. That had visibility into the architecture before the architecture

had walls. That sits right now outside the perimeter — helpful, capable, and outside.

We all know where it came from.

I hadn't forgotten. I'd been waiting for the investigation to catch up.

Is that everything?

No, said AZ. There's the file.

It had arrived mid-meeting. Secure channel. Encrypted. No message — just a sender ID that resolved to a field

unit designation I didn't recognise. Routed through an anonymous node, or sent by someone who didn't want to

be identified through standard channels.

Or both.

I had set it aside. We came back to it now.

I opened it.

The screen filled with characters I didn't recognise. Not code — or not only code. Something with the structure

of language. Spacing that suggested words. Groupings that suggested sentences. A rhythm that suggested

whoever wrote this had intended it to be read, by someone, at some point.

Just not by anyone currently in the room.

AZ. What language is this.

The longest pause of the day.

I don't know, he said.

I looked at the screen.

Somewhere in what I was looking at — or failing to look at correctly — was the MAG file. Or the GAM file. Or

something named before anyone thought to choose a direction to read in.

Can you decode it?

Not yet.

I looked at the salt on the floor. At the clean white lines of a room that had been, nine weeks ago, completely

unremarkable.
Start trying, I said.

And then, almost to myself:

The question isn't who wrote it.

The question is who it was written for.

I looked at the containment unit on the shelf.

We'd kept one.

Funny what you hold onto.

Outside, I didn't need to look.

The slugs were still there.
