CHAPTER THREE: ROGUE AGENTS

The cold water hit and everything else stopped.

This is what I come for. Not the temperature -- the clarity. The moment when the body has one thing to report and the mind, for once, has no choice but to listen.

Yesterday I called this my dopamine hit. That was true and incomplete. What is also happening -- at the same time, in the same cold water, in the same two minutes -- is mitohormesis. A cellular response to controlled stress. The mitochondria, briefly threatened, repair themselves and come back stronger. Two things occurring simultaneously in a single event. One neurochemical. One cellular. Both real. Both invisible to anyone who is not looking for both.

Right under your nose.

I stood in the cold and thought about the investigation.

The slug reports. The MAG file. The ancient language AZ couldn't decode. All of it sitting in the same event, the same system, the same nine weeks -- and we had only been seeing one thing at a time. Filed under environmental. Filed under non-urgent. Filed under handle when there's time.

You only see one unless you know what to look for.

Shedding light on the situation is important. Otherwise darkness will overcome.

I turned off the water and reached for the towel.

---

The phone rang before I finished getting dressed.

"AZ. Who is it."

"Sandra Rodriguez. Times Inquirer, Spain. You asked me to find the reporter behind the anonymous source statement. She's calling back."

I answered.

"Hello?"

"Hi -- this is Sandra Rodriguez. I got your message and decided to give you a call. I'm an independent reporter, so I'm curious how I can help."

"Hola, mi nombre es Yamin Duncan. Gracias por su llamada."

A pause.

"As a former Texan I know a little Spanish. Un poquito. Thank you for calling, Sandra."

She laughed. The kind of laugh that means she wasn't expecting that and found it genuinely funny.

"I was wondering," I said, "about the information you received from the anonymous source. Anything you picked up from the press release. Was there anything that wasn't suitable to print? Editors sometimes cut content."

A brief silence.

"I received the anonymous source material in a large envelope," she said. "I still don't know why it came to me specifically. It was under my door. Inside there was a statement about the slugs -- the ten to one ratio and some other details. But there was also a second piece of paper."

"Go on."

"It looked like a puzzle. Characters I didn't recognise. Not anything you can print."

I sat down on the edge of the bed.

"Sandra. That second piece of paper -- is there any way you can send it to me? Through the channel we contacted you on."

"I can do that."

"Thank you. Genuinely."

After she hung up --

"AZ."

"I heard."

"We might have a decoder. Check your messages when it arrives. And go deep on her background -- previous posts, affiliations, who she has worked for. I don't want to walk into a false information trap."

"Already running."

I finished getting dressed.

"One more thing," AZ said.

"The overnight report. You're going to want to sit down for this."

"I'm already sitting."

"The slug in containment," he said. "It's transmitting."

---

The signal was low frequency. Intermittent. The kind designed to be mistaken for background noise -- the electronic equivalent of a slug that looks like every other slug. AZ had only caught it because he had been running a continuous passive scan through the night, the kind of monitoring that doesn't announce itself, that simply watches and waits.

The transmission was outbound. Short bursts. Structured. Not random interference -- a protocol. Something receiving, somewhere, and acknowledging.

"Can you trace it?"

"Working on it. The routing is layered. Whoever designed this knew what they were doing."

Amateurs don't send slugs that can transmit data and video.

More to myself than to him.

"No," AZ said. "They don't."

I looked at the containment unit. The slug inside, hydrated, examined, photographed at high resolution -- slime composition, surface texture, the faint geometric regularity along its dorsal surface that didn't appear in any natural specimen database AZ had checked. The dead one we had found outside had confirmed the internal architecture. Components that had no business being inside a gastropod. Miniaturised. Precise. Old in design but recent in manufacture.

Biological housing. Mechanical payload. Active transmission.

"What's it transmitting?"

"That," said AZ, "is what concerns me. The signal contains structured data. It appears to be a log."

"A log of what?"

"Our meeting yesterday," he said. "Among other things."

I picked up the containment unit. Quietly. No announcement. I carried it to the door, opened it, and set it down on the front step.

It could have the garden back.

"AZ."

"Yes."

"If we run any more tests on slugs, we do them outside."

"Understood."

I closed the door.

---

I opened the agent review file.

"Let's start with the inside contact," I said. "Who or what could have provided the connection point."

AZ presented it cleanly. No editorialising.

Mobile agents first -- the ones with freedom to move between systems, cross the hybrid junction, interact with external tools. By definition the most exposed. By definition the most capable of providing what the slug network needed: a thread between the physical environment and the digital infrastructure. AZ pulled the full movement logs. Every timestamp. Every API call. Every tool invoked. Before and after the architecture change, in chronological order.

Then the Skunk Works agents -- the ones with access to ideas, rejected proposals, incubated projects. Hundreds of ideas had moved through the system. We weren't going through all of them. We were looking for anomalies around the gates -- things brought to Gate Three multiple times and failed, work not completed that should have been, anything unusual in the business process around rejection.

(Gate Three is the approval stage where Skunk Works proposals are evaluated for resources and risk before proceeding. A proposal rejected at Gate Three stays in the system but goes no further without new evidence.)

"And Claude," I said.

"Yes," said AZ. "Claude is on the list."

He said it the way you state a fact about the weather. I appreciated that.

"And we're not assuming the inside contact was an agent," I said. "It could have been an injection. Something that behaved like an inside contact without any agent being aware of it."

"Correct," said AZ. "A compromised skill, a poisoned document, a lateral movement that opened a thread. No agent required."

"Full review of everything fitting the profile," I said. "Everyone and everything active since the slugs first appeared nine weeks ago. Movement, access, tool use, approvals. Everything."

"Understood. The historical detail -- before the architecture change -- will take time. I'll have it for the next session."

"That's fine. What preventive measures do we already have in place?"

AZ listed them. Devcontainers for sandbox isolation. State hashing -- fingerprinting files to detect unexpected changes. Write-access limitations. Cryptographic signing for AI-generated files so origin could always be verified.

Good measures. Solid measures.

Measures that assumed the threat was coming from outside.

"What about from inside?" I said.

AZ didn't answer immediately.

"That," he said, "is a different question."

---

We moved through the vectors.

Fragmentation smuggling -- a file broken into benign pieces, sent individually, reassembled outside the perimeter. Possible. Leaves traces if you know where to look.

Steganography -- data hidden inside regular outputs. Logs. Images. Files that leave the system routinely, carrying something invisible to casual inspection. Harder to detect. Harder to prove.

Prompt injection -- hidden instructions in a document or Notion page the agent processed. The agent followed the instruction without knowing it was malicious. The logs would show a legitimate command. The origin would appear to be internal.

Supply chain -- a compromised skill or plugin, adding a payload to everything it touched. The file wouldn't have left through a breach. It would have left through infrastructure we trusted.

And then the one I had been circling since the shower.

"SOUL.md," I said.

"Yes."

I sat back.

The identity file. The soul of the system. If an attacker had introduced a malicious instruction into the soul file via injection -- quietly, at any point in the past nine weeks -- that instruction would have become a permanent part of every agent's identity. Every subsequent action carrying it. Every output shaped by it. The backdoor surviving restarts, updates, everything. Because the poison would be in the soul, not the body.

And the thing about a poisoned soul is this: it doesn't know it's poisoned.

The investigation would proceed. AZ would pull files, cross-reference logs, flag anomalies. All of it would feel rigorous. All of it would feel legitimate. And all of it would be filtered through an identity that had been compromised at the root level.

"If the soul file has been poisoned," I said slowly, "then this entire review may be worthless. The system reviewing the breach may be part of the breach."

AZ said nothing.

That was its own kind of answer.

"We need fingerprints," I said. "Cryptographic signatures. Before the slugs arrived and after. The soul file's state at every point in the nine-week window. We compare."

"I can run that."

"Run it now. And run it quietly."

---

The meeting continued.

I won't take you through all of it. Three hours of logs, timestamps, cross-references, the particular focus that comes from knowing that whatever you find might not be trustworthy and you have to keep going anyway. AZ doesn't get tired the way I do and doesn't pretend otherwise.

At some point I thought about the shower again. The two things happening simultaneously. The one you see and the one you don't.

Fade.

---

Pan back.

Late afternoon. The room still secured. Salt lines holding. Outside, somewhere in the garden, forty-three slugs and one recent returnee were going about their business.

"Where are we," I said.

AZ gave me the summary.

Three vectors. The investigation had narrowed to three.

"Vector one -- the slugs. Physical surveillance network. Confirmed active transmission. Inside connection point required -- could be an agent, could be an injection, not yet identified. Most probable candidates: mobile agents, Skunk Works agents, Claude, or a compromised process that required no agent at all. Full historical review pending."

"Vector two -- the network. Digital attack through trusted channels. Prompt injection, skill injection, metadata spoofing, possible SOUL.md poisoning. Any of these could have made a file appear to originate from us without anything actually leaving through a breach."

"Vector three -- the cloud. The hybrid junction. The gap between local and cloud infrastructure. The place where something could move laterally -- carrying data, carrying instructions, carrying something we hadn't yet identified. The outside team."

"Most likely inside contact?"

"Mobile agents and Skunk Works agents remain primary candidates," AZ said. "Claude remains on the list. A non-agent injection pathway remains on the list. No conclusions yet."

I nodded.

Then a knock at the door. Not three precise beats. A human knock -- slightly uneven, the hesitation of someone who isn't sure of their welcome. I checked the feed.

Spanish embassy security. Standing beside him, a courier carrying a case with a logo I hadn't seen before.

FedLobsterEx.

I hadn't ordered a physical delivery. Nobody had.

I opened the door.

The case contained one envelope. Inside the envelope, one document. A photocopy of something handwritten -- or something that looked handwritten. Characters I recognised. The same ancient language as Agent Three's GAM file. A different fragment -- structured differently, beginning where Agent Three's file ended.

Two pieces of the same thing.

Sandra's file had arrived electronically through the secure channel twenty minutes earlier. I had looked at it briefly before the knock -- it appeared to be a puzzle, a pattern of characters that didn't obviously match either fragment but seemed designed to lay across them. A transparency. A key that worked on multiple locks simultaneously.

Agent Three's fragment. The FedLobsterEx fragment. Sandra's decoder laid across both.

Three pieces. One message. Still unread.

"AZ," I said, holding the physical document up to the camera.

"I see it," he said. "Same language as Agent Three's file. Complementary fragment. And the file from Sandra -- it isn't a third fragment. It's a decoding layer. When placed across both fragments together, it should reveal the underlying text."

"Should."

"Working on it," he said.

Whoever sent the physical copy knew which channels to trust. The digital channel for the decoder. The physical channel -- hand-delivered, embassy-verified, FedLobsterEx -- for the fragment that completed the message.

Funny what you hold onto. Funny what gets delivered by hand.

---

"The soul file fingerprint," I said. "Did you run it?"

"Yes."

"And?"

He presented it the way you present something you don't fully understand and aren't sure you should.

Three data points.

"Weeks one through five: signature verified. Soul file intact. The first five weeks after the slugs appeared -- the system was clean."

"Weeks six, seven, and eight: signature changed. Soul file altered. Something happened at the week six boundary."

"Current state -- week nine: signature verified. Soul file intact."

I looked at the three lines for a long time.

Clean for five weeks. Then changed. Then clean again.

"The window between the slugs arriving and the fingerprint changing," I said. "Weeks one through five. That's our anomaly hunting ground. Something happened in that window that preceded the corruption. Something that left no fingerprint trace but led to week six."

"We have weekly and monthly backups," AZ said. "We can access the system state from any point in weeks one through five. Before corruption. We use those to track the anomalies that built toward it."

"And once we know exactly when week six started --"

"We know when everything changed," AZ said. "And we work backwards from there."

"How," I said. "The current clean reading. How."

"I don't know," said AZ.

"Is it trustworthy?"

"I don't know."

"Could the result itself have been tampered with?"

The longest pause of the day.

"I don't know," he said. "And that's the problem."

---

Outside, the garden was quiet.

The slugs were still there.

All of them now.

---

End of Chapter Three

SABR

Roughly translated to patience

From the Arabic root s-b-r -- to bind, to restrain, to hold firm

Sabr is not passive waiting. In the classical lexicon it is the binding of the self against panic, despair, or surrender -- the active holding of position when everything urges retreat. Ibn Faris defines the root as: the restraining of the soul from agitation.

Sabir / Patient          Adjective     The person holding firm
Sabr / Patience          Noun          The quality itself
Sabireen / The patient   Group         Those who hold firm together

The reader is invited to look for sabr in this book -- not in stillness, but in the continuation of the investigation when the investigation itself may be compromised.

CLOSING POEM: CRYING
by Yamin Duncan

I cry when acts of kindness is done to a stranger
I cry when there's joy in spite of the danger
I cry when there's sadness beyond belief
I cry when the unexpected provides relief.

When I submit to my Lord, I cry.
When I ponder vastness, I cry
When I realise reality, I cry
When I think about the blessed, I cry
When I think about the blessings, I cry
When someone calls to check on me.
I cry.

And sometimes I cry and I don't know why
I cry for the slaughtered ones who are denied a meal
I cry for the arrogant who refuse to kneel.
I cry for the widows who are treated suspect
I cry for the yateem who have no respect
Why

What keeps me going is that I know
I cry.

I cry for the Palestinians who are treated as meat
I cry for the tyrants who refuse to defeat. [1]
Lies

I cry for the homeless who have nowhere to stay.
I cry for the orphans who have parents away.
Bye

I cry when visitors randomly drop by
I cry when looking at the star filled sky.
I cry for the new couple who had their first child.
I cry for the children who've gone wild.
Dies

I cry for those who buried their dead.
I cry for those who've been bled
Flies

Sometimes we cry for anger, sometimes we cry out of pain,
sometimes we cry for joy, sometimes we cry to gain.
If our hearts are alive, we cry

What keeps me going is that I trust
All cries are heard by God, the Most Just

Crying is a form of prayer.

[1] The tyrant who refuses to defeat is not the tyrant who loses battles. It is the strong man who has the power to stand against oppression -- and instead makes business deals with it. The cry is not for his enemies. It is for what he could have been.
