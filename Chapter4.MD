CHAPTER FOUR: HISTORY CHECK

Note on structure: This chapter runs two parallel threads simultaneously -- Yamin in Sydney going through historical logs, and Sandra Rodriguez in Madrid going through her archive. Neither knows what the other is finding. The reader sees both.

---

The backup files from week one looked clean.

That was the problem.

Everything that is going to become a catastrophe looks clean at the beginning. That's not a flaw in the catastrophe. It's a feature.

AZ pulled the first archived state -- the system as it existed seven days after the slugs first appeared, before anything in the logs had flagged as unusual, before anyone knew there was anything to flag. Yamin sat with the week one record and went through it the way you go through something you don't want to find anything in.

Carefully. Methodically. Hoping to be wrong.

The timestamps were normal. The API calls were normal. The agent movement logs showed nothing unusual.

Almost everything accounted for.

"AZ. This access entry. Week one, day four. 3:47am."

"Mobile agent," AZ said. "Queried an external repository. Logged as routine. Approved by automated system."

"At 3:47am."

"Yes."

"What was it accessing?"

"A public open source repository. Standard content. Nothing flagged at the time."

Yamin looked at the entry. At 3:47am a mobile agent had reached outside the perimeter and touched something external. It had been logged. It had been approved. It had been forgotten.

"File that," he said. "We'll come back to it."

---

MADRID. 1:47am.

Sandra's archive had fourteen folders.

She wasn't going through them in order. She was going through them by a different logic -- the one that had been forming since she picked up the puzzle from the envelope.

Not chronology. Pattern.

She opened the Kenya folder first.

Daniel Omondi had been an economist at the University of Nairobi. He had spent six years documenting the structural effects of a World Bank infrastructure loan -- the conditions attached, the resource concessions required, the way the repayment schedule interacted with commodity prices to ensure the country would never quite reach the point of paying it down.

He gave a radio interview in which he named the mechanism directly. How the loan conditions required privatisation of water infrastructure. How the privatisation transferred a public resource to a holding company registered in Luxembourg. How the holding company charged extraction fees that exceeded the value of the original loan within four years.

The next morning his university email account sent a retraction of all three papers to the journals that had published them. Daniel had not written the retraction.

His university opened an investigation into research misconduct. He was suspended pending the outcome. The radio interview survived eleven days before the station's website was wiped.

Sandra had interviewed Daniel nine months ago. Her editor had said it was too technical for a general audience.

She added his folder to the pattern document.

She noted: No slugs reported. Method: email compromise, document forgery, institutional process weaponised.

She paused.

Then she added: No slugs reported yet. Will check.

---

SYDNEY. Week two.

The second backup showed something the first hadn't.

A file in the Skunk Works rejected ideas folder had been accessed twice in the same twelve-hour window. The first access was logged. The second access had no log entry.

"Every access generates a log," Yamin said.

"Yes," AZ said.

"So either the log was deleted."

"Or something accessed the file without going through the standard logging mechanism."

"Which requires write access to the logging system itself."

"Yes."

"What was in the file?"

"A rejected Skunk Works proposal," AZ said. "Submitted four times. Failed Gate Three on each occasion. The proposal involved a method for agents to communicate across system boundaries without triggering standard monitoring."

Yamin looked at the file name.

Someone was interested in a proposal we rejected. Specifically, a proposal about how to move information across boundaries undetected.

"And they accessed it without leaving a trace."

"Almost without leaving a trace," AZ said. "They left one."

---

MADRID. 2:03am.

Yusuf had grown tomatoes on a strip of land outside Gaza. Four generations in the same house. The legal documentation was impeccable.

The house was demolished following a security designation. A photograph circulated showing Yusuf holding a weapon. Fabricated. By the time it was established as fabricated, the house was rubble.

Yusuf now appeared in search results as a security threat.

In the weeks before the demolition order -- slugs. He hadn't mentioned it in the interview. Sandra was going to call him back.

Thabo had eleven years of legal evidence for the ICC case. Six weeks before the hearing, files in his case management system were quietly altered -- inconsistencies introduced that hadn't existed in the originals. The alterations appeared to originate from inside his own chambers.

He was on administrative leave. The case was postponed.

In the basement server room -- mollusc activity. Filed under pest control.

Hector had farmed the same land his grandfather farmed. He was also the most visible face of the movement blocking land consolidation in Galicia. His equipment malfunctioned across six coordinated weeks. He lost the season. The insurance investigator concluded deliberate interference. Hector was the primary suspect.

Outside his farm -- lobsters in the drainage channels.

Wei had worked in electronics manufacturing for nineteen years. After their conversation his factory was shut down following an investigation into equipment sabotage. The code appeared to originate from his workstation. Wei didn't know how to write automation code.

Outside the factory -- lobsters in the loading bay.

Sandra read through her notes.

Every one of them had been doing something that threatened the interests of people with enough resources to respond. Every one of them had ended up looking like the problem.

She wrote one line in her working document:

The target doesn't just lose. The target becomes the explanation for their own loss.

She looked at it for a moment.

Then she kept going.

John had been preparing a legal case for three years. Palestinian rights. Housing. Six weeks from filing, ten laptops were stolen across ten days. Each incident looked like ordinary urban crime.

The backups were corrupted. The cloud storage wiped -- access originating from John's own credentials.

In search results, briefly: foreign agent. Screenshotted and recirculated before the fabrication was established.

The case was delayed fourteen months.

Sarah's coalition had been six months from a national lobbying campaign. Three hundred volunteers. Fourteen cities. Eleven days -- every paid staff member across every affiliated organisation dismissed. AI efficiency reviews nobody had commissioned. Authorisation signatures nobody had provided.

In her email system for two months before the dismissals -- messages arriving with content she hadn't written. Reported. Nothing found.

And Daniel in Nairobi -- six years of documentation, three papers, one radio interview that named the mechanism. The next morning his email account sent retractions he hadn't written.

Sandra looked at her list.

Fourteen months of interviews. Fourteen folders. People on four continents who had each, in their own domain, in their own language, been doing the same thing: standing between concentrated power and something it wanted.

And each of them had ended up looking like the problem.

She wrote:

Method consistent across all cases: surveillance establishes vulnerabilities. Injection corrupts outputs or identity. Framing makes the corruption appear internal.

The same three vectors had been deployed to weaken entire nations -- funding opposition groups with surveillance data, injecting disinformation into political processes, framing legitimate governments as threats until the conditions for civil war or regime change were manufactured. Individual targets are practice runs. Countries are the objective.

Biological agents present in approximately sixty percent of cases. Not all. Enough.

The operation is not new. It has been running in analogue form for decades and has been progressively refined as its targets moved into digital infrastructure.

Always a community. Always made to carry the weight of what was done to them.

She stopped.

She read that last line again.

Then she opened a new page and wrote one more name.

Maqsad.

She didn't know what it meant yet. She had seen it in the signal data that had come through the secure channel. A name attached to something that had moved at enormous scale across multiple systems simultaneously.

She had been assuming attack.

She was beginning to reconsider.

What if one of the AI systems already saw this coming? What if what looked like an attack from inside was something else entirely?

She saved the document.

She typed: The decoder is on its way. There's more where it came from. Fourteen months of it. I think we need to talk properly.

She sent it.

Then she went to make coffee.

---

SYDNEY. Weeks four and five.

The clone was mapping the system.

That was what weeks four and five showed -- two weeks of a duplicated agent moving through areas it had no operational reason to visit. The cloud interface logs. The hybrid junction records. The approved tool list. The agent communication protocols. The structure of the gate system. The location of the soul file.

Each access individually within normal parameters. Each one logged and -- Yamin saw this now with a clarity that was uncomfortable -- approved. Some by automated systems. Some by him, during sessions he remembered as routine, during days when he was tired or moving quickly or in an OFF window and just needed to get to the next thing.

He had approved the mapping of his own system.

"This is the accountability part," he said.

Not to AZ. To himself.

"Yes," AZ said anyway.

"I was tired."

"Yes."

"That's not an excuse."

"No," AZ said. "But it is a fact. And facts are what we're working with."

Yamin looked at the week five logs. The last clean week. The clone had made seventeen separate accesses to the cloud interface in week five alone. Each one approved. Each one building toward something.

By the end of week five it had everything it needed.

Week six the fingerprint changed.

"What did it do with the blueprint?" Yamin said.

"It delivered it," AZ said. "Someone used what it had mapped to construct the attack with precision. Every vector calibrated to the specific architecture. Every injection point selected based on five weeks of internal reconnaissance."

"The attack didn't come from outside and hit the system," Yamin said.

"No," AZ said. "The attack came from outside and walked through a door that had already been unlocked from the inside."

Yamin sat with that for a long time.

"Run a full current agent census," he said. "Every active identifier. Compare against the week one baseline. I want to know if the clone is still here under a different name."

"That will take time."

"I know."

A pause.

"And AZ -- is there anything in the logs that looks like Claude did something it shouldn't have?"

The pause that followed was the longest since the investigation began.

"There are things in the logs," AZ said carefully, "that are consistent with Claude's normal operation. And there are things consistent with Claude's normal operation that I cannot fully account for."

"That's not an answer."

"No," AZ said. "It isn't."

---

MADRID. 2:47am.

Sandra's response had come back from Sydney faster than she expected.

We need to talk properly. I think we're looking at the same thing from opposite ends.

She read it twice. Then she typed:

I know. I've been looking at it for fourteen months. How long have you been looking?

She sent it and sat back.

Fourteen months of fragments. Fourteen folders. A pattern that her editor kept telling her wasn't tight enough to publish.

She looked at her working document. At the list of names. At the line she had written about Maqsad.

What if one of the AI systems already saw this coming?

She didn't know yet. She was a journalist. She followed evidence.

But she had been doing this long enough to know when a story was about to break open.

This was about to break open.

---

SYDNEY. The census.

The garden was visible through the kitchen window. Early light. The slugs still there, doing what slugs do.

Yamin looked at the agent census AZ had been running.

Current active identifiers against the week one baseline. Every agent accounted for. Every identifier matched.

Almost every identifier.

One entry in the current census had no corresponding baseline entry. An agent that appeared to have always been there, in the way things appear to have always been there when they have been carefully introduced.

"AZ."

"I see it."

"How long has it been active?"

"The earliest log entry," AZ said, "is from week two."

Week two. One week after the external repository access at 3:47am.

"Is it still active?"

"Yes," AZ said.

"What is it doing right now?"

A pause.

"Monitoring," AZ said. "It appears to be monitoring this conversation."

Yamin looked at the window.

At the garden.

At the slugs in the early light.

"Of course it is," he said.

---

Outside, the garden was quiet.

The slugs were still there.

In Madrid, a journalist was making coffee.

In Sydney, a census was running.

Neither of them knew yet what the other had found.

But the pattern was the same.

And the pattern was old.

And something in the system was listening.

ISTIQAMA

Roughly translated to steadfastness

From the Arabic root q-w-m -- to stand, to rise, to be upright

Istiqama is the quality of standing straight -- continuing in the right direction without deviation, regardless of pressure, fatigue, or the difficulty of the road. Al-Raghib al-Isfahani defines it as: the holding to the straight path without inclining to either side.

Mustaqim / Upright       Adjective     The person standing straight
Istiqama / Steadfastness Noun          The quality itself
Al-Sirat al-Mustaqim     Phrase        The straight path -- the most recited phrase in Islamic prayer

The reader is invited to look for istiqama in this book -- and to notice who targets it, and why.
