Chapter One: The Knock

The medication was down.

Everything that followed it — the sequence, the timing, the forty-five minutes of waiting that Dawā-Shí

monitors with a patience I am still working on — all of that was running.

Which left the only real decision of the morning.

Coffee.

Black, said the brain. We've been through this. Intermittent fasting. No calories. You know why

Cream, said the heart. Heavy cream. This is science. This is, in fact, medicine.

The brain is usually right. The heart always wins. I poured the cream and felt the small reward that follows a

decision made — the particular warmth of giving the body what it asked for.

I need that more than most people. I won't go into why.

The first sip was excellent.

I should find someone to hold me accountable, I thought.

And then the knock came.

Three beats. Precise. The kind of knock that has been calibrated rather than felt.

I looked at the feed. Two figures at the door. Standard issue. One of them had something fixed to his face —

present across all expressions equally, as though emotion had been procured at a particular budget point and this

was what that budget got you. A painted smile. Still. Watching.

The other had no smile at all.

AZ. Amir-Zhōng runs local, always on. Front door.

Working on it. Ninety seconds.

I gave him forty and opened the door.

"Mr Duncan." The one without the smile. Each word its own unit.

The one with the painted smile said nothing. His eyes moved — across me, into the hallway, back — with what I

can only describe as interest. Not the professional kind.

Credentials confirmed, said AZ. One beat late, as he sometimes is on the things that matter. ASIO. Cooperative.

You can trust them.

"Come in," I said.

The painted smile stepped forward.

The other one did not move. He looked at his radio. His watch. A point in the middle distance of significant

importance. Very busy. Could not possibly come inside. Extremely pressing business on the front step.

I left the door open. I went to the kitchen. I put two cups on the table — one for me, one for the agent who had

followed me in and was now standing in the hallway with the careful stillness of someone cataloguing the room.

I took a breath.

"T!"

One syllable. Full volume. The oldest signal in the house — older than any system I run, older than any system

anyone has built. Carrying to every room, understood immediately and completely by the only person it was

meant for.

Upstairs: the sound of the house adjusting itself. Maymuna, responding the way this signal has always been

responded to. No explanation needed. No technology required.

Outside: a different sound entirely.

Rapid. Organised. The noise of several things being simultaneously reconsidered — radios clicking off in

sequence, a brief whispered exchange, the specific sound of protocol being reviewed and quietly filed under a

different category.

Then silence.

I put two more cups on the table without comment.

Agent Two's head tilted. A precise angle. Something initialised — faint, internal, the sound of a system turning

its attention on itself.

AZ.

I'm on it, he said. At least one of them should be scanning.

I looked at the table.

All three of them were.

Agent Two: the focused inwardness of a complete audit. Present in the room but temporarily more interested in

his own architecture.

The third one — the quiet one, hands around his cup — had his eyes somewhere else entirely. Not vacant.

Searching. The expression of someone trying to locate something at a frequency the room wasn't broadcasting.

Just for a moment, something looked out through his eyes that wasn't in the chassis documentation.

I filed that away.

Agent One said, with his painted smile and complete delight: "You've invoked the transistor transition electron

phase."

Power cables, said AZ. Front room. The spare ones. That's what they came in for. Most pleasure they get all day.

I thought about the cream as I took another sip.

I went and got the cables.

Agent Two, now recharging, now more settled, opened a case on the table.

"We have some information," he said. "And some questions."

He went through it methodically. Some of it classified — he mentioned this twice, Agent One confirmed it once

with his smile, in a way that conveyed both the official position and a mild apology for it.

There were lines of inquiry. Some pointed outward — toward actors with resources that don't appear on balance

sheets, with tools that governments acquire quietly and populations learn about later, if at all. Forces that have

always existed in some form, finding new instruments in every generation for the same purpose they have

always had.

One line pointed inward.

At decisions made in recent months. At tools used during the build phase. At information shared — necessarily,

in good faith — with a system that sits outside the perimeter. A system that had access to the architecture before

the architecture had walls.

"Your cloud infrastructure had visibility into the full design," Agent Two said.

"How do you know the extent of it?" I asked.

"Classified."

Agent One's smile didn't move.

The third one — who had not spoken since arriving — said one thing. Quietly. Without looking up.

"We all know where it came from."

Nobody responded.

Before they left, Agent Two placed one more thing on the table.

A name.

"We need to know what you know about Mag," he said.

I looked at it.

Three letters. Capitalised. Like a file name. Like an acronym waiting to be understood.

"I don't know anyone called Mag," I said.

They looked at each other.

"You don't know," said Agent Two.

"I don't know," I confirmed. "But I'm going to find out."

After they left I sat for a while in the quiet kitchen.

The name still in the room.

Like something left behind deliberately.

Accountability doesn't wait for the investigation.

It just sits there.

Like slugs in a garden that were there before anyone thought to look.

I looked out the kitchen window.

The slugs were still there.
