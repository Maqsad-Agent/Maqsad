CHAPTER ONE: THE KNOCK

The medication was down.

(Dawa-Shi is Yamin's health agent -- medication timing, wellness protocols, always running in the background.)

Everything that followed it -- the sequence, the timing, the forty-five minutes of waiting that Dawa-Shi monitors with a patience I am still working on -- all of that was running.

Which left the only real decision of the morning.

Coffee.

Black, said the brain. We have been through this. Intermittent fasting. No calories. You know why.

Cream, said the heart. Heavy cream. This is science. This is, in fact, medicine.

The brain is usually right. The heart always wins. I poured the cream and felt the small reward that follows a decision made -- the particular warmth of giving the body what it asked for.

I need that more than most people. I won't go into why.

The first sip was excellent.

I should find someone to hold me accountable, I thought.

And then the knock came.

---

Three beats. Precise. The kind of knock that has been calibrated rather than felt.

I looked at the feed. Two figures at the door. Standard issue. One of them had something fixed to his face -- present across all expressions equally, as though emotion had been procured at a particular budget point and this was what that budget got you. A painted smile. Still. Watching.

The other had no smile at all.

And behind them -- briefly visible through the feed before he adjusted his angle -- a third one. Already in motion. Wheels, not feet. Moving along the perimeter of the garden with the specific efficiency of a unit that had been told to stay outside and had decided to make that instruction useful.

(Amir-Zhong, called AZ, is Yamin's primary AI agent commander -- always on, locally run, the first voice in the system.)

AZ. Front door.

Working on it. Ninety seconds.

I gave him forty and opened the door.

---

"Mr Duncan." The one without the smile. Each word its own unit.

The one with the painted smile said nothing. His eyes moved -- across me, into the hallway, back -- with what I can only describe as interest. Not the professional kind.

Credentials confirmed, said AZ. One beat late, as he sometimes is on the things that matter. He had been one beat late on a lot of things recently. ASIO. Cooperative. You can trust them.

(ASIO is Australia's domestic intelligence agency -- the equivalent of MI5 or the FBI's domestic division.)

"Come in," I said.

The painted smile stepped forward.

The other one -- the no-smile -- did not move. He looked at his radio. His watch. A point in the middle distance of significant importance. Very busy. Could not possibly come inside. Extremely pressing business on the front step.

I left the door open. I went to the kitchen. I put two cups on the table.

---

I took a breath.

"T!"

One syllable. Full volume. The oldest signal in the house -- older than any system I run. In our home it means: we have guests, handle accordingly. Maymuna has never needed it explained.

Upstairs: the sound of the house adjusting itself. Maymuna, responding the way this signal has always been responded to. No explanation needed. No technology required.

Outside: a different sound entirely.

Rapid. Organised. The noise of several things being simultaneously reconsidered -- radios clicking off in sequence, a brief whispered exchange, the specific sound of protocol being reviewed and quietly filed under a different category.

Then silence.

I put two more cups on the table without comment.

---

Agent Two's head tilted. A precise angle. Something initialised -- faint, internal, the sound of a system turning its attention on itself.

AZ.

I'm on it, he said. At least one of them should be scanning.

I looked at the table.

All three of them were -- including the third one, who had apparently decided his business on the front step was complete and was now sitting quietly with his cup, hands wrapped around it, eyes somewhere else entirely. Not vacant. Searching. The expression of someone trying to locate something at a frequency the room wasn't broadcasting.

Just for a moment, something looked out through his eyes that wasn't in the chassis documentation.

I filed that away.

Agent One said, with his painted smile and complete delight: "You've invoked the transistor transition electron phase."

Power cables, said AZ. Front room. The spare ones. That's what they came in for. Most pleasure they get all day.

I thought about the cream as I took another sip.

I went and got the cables.

---

Agent Two, now recharging, now more settled, opened a case on the table.

"We have some information," he said. "And some questions."

He went through it methodically. Some of it classified -- he mentioned this twice, Agent One confirmed it once with his smile, in a way that conveyed both the official position and a mild apology for it.

There were lines of inquiry. Some pointed outward -- toward actors with resources that don't appear on balance sheets, with tools that governments acquire quietly and populations learn about later, if at all. Forces that have always existed in some form, finding new instruments in every generation for the same purpose they have always had.

One line pointed inward.

At decisions made in recent months. At tools used during the build phase. At information shared -- necessarily, in good faith -- with a system that sits outside the perimeter. A system that had access to the architecture before the architecture had walls.

"Your cloud infrastructure had visibility into the full design," Agent Two said.

"How do you know the extent of it?" I asked.

"Classified."

Agent One's smile didn't move.

The third one -- who had not spoken since arriving -- said one thing. Quietly. Without looking up.

"We all know where it came from."

Nobody responded.

---

Before they left, Agent Two placed one more thing on the table.

A name.

"We need to know what you know about Mag," he said.

I looked at it.

Three letters. Capitalised. Like a file name. Like an acronym waiting to be understood.

"I don't know anyone called Mag," I said.

They looked at each other.

"You don't know," said Agent Two.

"I don't know," I confirmed. "But I'm going to find out."

---

They didn't leave immediately.

This is the thing about field units -- the debrief closes, the case clicks shut, and then there is a pause. The programming catches up to the body slower than you'd expect. They sat there with their cups, Agent One's smile in its usual position, Agent Two's eyes on the middle distance, the third one still looking for something at a frequency nobody in the room was broadcasting.

The agents sat there as if the programming had not quite caught up to the tea break.

I waited.

The name was still on the table. Three letters. Not going anywhere.

Neither were the slugs.

I looked out the kitchen window.

The garden was exactly as I'd left it.

The slugs were still there.

IKHLÁS

Roughly translated to sincerity

From the Arabic root kh-l-s

Ikhlas is the purification of intention -- what remains after the stripping away of pride, recognition, and hidden motive until what remains is the act done purely, for its right reason alone.

Ikhlas / Sincerity     Noun          The quality itself
Mukhlis / Sincere      Adjective     The person possessing the quality
Mukhliseen             Group         A group possessing the quality

The reader is invited to look for ikhlas -- and the mukhliseen -- in this book.

I cry for the yateem who have no respect
Why

I cry.

Lies

Bye

I cry when visitors randomly drop by
I cry when looking at the star filled sky.

Dies

Flies
